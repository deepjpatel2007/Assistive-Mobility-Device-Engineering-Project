# Design Process

## Source Deliverables
This file extracts and organizes design-process information from the original ENGG1100 Teddy Bear Wheelchair prototype and final reports.

## Design Objective
The team prioritized speed, stability, reliability, and feasibility. The prototype was developed to perform strongly in the precision speed run while still meeting safety, mass, and launch requirements.

## Early Design Approach
The project began by separating the design into major subsystems:

- Chassis and mounting
- Electronics
- Launcher
- Mechanical drive system
- Aesthetics and body design

Team discussions were kept open so members from any subsystem could suggest changes to other parts of the design.

## Major Design Decisions

### Chassis Layout
The final chassis selection was a three-wheel, widthwise baseplate configuration. This option was selected because it had the highest weighted decision score and was the most feasible with the available parts.

### Chassis Decision Matrix Summary

| Option | Description | Total Rating | Weighted Score |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| Option A | 4 wheels, baseplate lengthwise | 25 | 4.8 |
| Option B | 4 wheels, baseplate widthwise | 26 | 5.3 |
| Option C | 3 wheels, baseplate widthwise | 31 | 6.4 |

## Why the Final Chassis Was Selected
The team originally considered four-wheel layouts, but axle length and available material constraints made them less feasible. The final three-wheel widthwise design reduced weight and rolling resistance while remaining practical to build with the provided resources.

## Drivetrain Decision
The team used a front-wheel-driven system powered by one motor mounted behind the front axle. A pulley system transferred power to the axle. This reduced mass compared with a two-motor design and helped improve speed.

## Launcher Decision
The team selected a mousetrap-based launcher with a servo-controlled trigger. This was chosen because it was simple, available, lightweight, and easy to trigger autonomously.

## Electronics Decision
The team used an Arduino-based control system. Instead of relying on a sensor, the design used timed Arduino control because the course was static and could be repeatedly tested.

## Aesthetic Design Decision
The final body design used an ambulance theme, called the “Ambearlance.” This design was selected because it was practical to build from cardboard, avoided copyrighted themes, provided space for the teddy bear, and allowed easy access to electronics through a hinged roof.

## Key Iterations
- Removed the second rear wheel to reduce weight and accommodate axle limitations.
- Mounted the breadboard vertically to save internal space.
- Adjusted the front axle and pulley system to improve motor-to-wheel power transfer.
- Refined Arduino timing after testing under competition conditions.
- Changed the launcher projectile from a bottle to a bottle-cap projectile for better distance.

## Engineering Lessons
The design process showed the importance of feasibility, mass control, mechanical alignment, subsystem integration, and repeated testing under realistic conditions.
